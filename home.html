A UX Analysis of the AmazonBasics Remote-Controlled Tower Fan

Product Description and Diagram

Product Overview

  • Oscillating Tower Fan
  • Extensive Settings
  • Includes Remote
  • Icon-Based Light-up Display
  • 1-7 hour Timer with Binary Display (1h, 2h, 4h switches)

Settings

Power Off • On
Mode Normal • Natural • Night
Strength Low • Medium • High
Swivel / Oscillation Off • On
Timer Off • 1-7 Hours

User Interviews and Observations

The Users: Key Observations

User 1

Aimed to make use of the fan's extensive settings. Used copious trial & error in order to find the perfect configuration.

User 2

Found the interface largely unfamiliar, unclear, and confusing. Strongly preferred the on-fan interface over the remote.

User 3

Owns a similar fan, albeit with a somewhat different interface. Was able to master the interface quickly, with minimal confusion.

Interview Questions

On a scale from 1 (least) to 10 (most), how approachable did you initially find the interface? Why?

7. I was pretty comfortable with the look of it, but I knew I would probably get some things wrong at first.

5. There were only five buttons, so I felt that it would not be too challenging to learn. But, it was unclear what each button's purpose was, and it didn't help that two of them look the same.

7. It mostly fit my expectations, but there were a couple of buttons that were a mystery to me at first.

Compared to your initial impression of the interface, did any part(s) of the interface catch you by surprise? If so, which part(s) and why?

I'm still not quite sure why the "mode" setting is necessary, but I wouldn't say I was "suprised" by it per se. Before you told me, I couldn't really tell what that button was doing.

The display was smaller than I thought it would be, and I was not expecting it to turn off after I didn't press any buttons for a few seconds. The way the timer was displayed felt weird too.

I was pleasantly surprised that when I turned it off and on again, it kept the same settings I had before.

On a scale from 1 (easy) to 10 (hard), how difficult was it to quickly grasp the fan's functionality? Why?

6. I had to go through the settings a couple times to get the hang of it. I wish the display had words on it instead of icons that I needed to interpret.

6. There wasn't a ton to grasp, but I couldn't tell what some of the icons meant. And to be honest, I would still have to guess which button is which [between "strength" and "mode"].

3. It wasn't too bad at all, other than the buttons being hard to tell apart. I was able to grasp everything within a minute or so.

Were there any specific elements of the interface that made it easy to achieve your desired settings? If so, which one(s) and why?

Overall, it wasn't too bad once I got the hang of it. But, at first it took a little more work than I expected.

The timer and swivel buttons were pretty obvious, but the timer was pretty much ruined by the way it's displayed.

Nothing in particular, although, like I said before, it's nice that my settings were maintained after I turned it off.

Were there any specific elements of the interface that made it difficult to achieve your desired settings? If so, which one(s) and why?

Before you told me what each icon meant, I had to put my hand in front of the fan to test for myself, since the icons were unclear. Especially for the "mode" setting.

The icons were so small that they were hard to see from across the room when I was using the remote. Also, the display turned off quickly so I couldn't see my settings.

The timer should not be a cycle; I overshot the time I wanted and had to press the button 7 more times to decrease it by an hour.

Were there any differences between your experience using the on-fan interface, and using the remote? If so, please elaborate.

Not really. If I had to think of something, when I turned on the oscillation with the button on the fan, the buttons of course started moving, which made it a little harder to use.

I like the idea of the remote, but it was harder to use because I couldn't see the icons very well from across the room. The remote should have its own display of the current settings.

The remote is a really nice feature. I didn't find either one too different from the other.

Are there any changes to the interface that you would suggest? If so, which one(s) and why?

The display, and the buttons, should definitely have words instead of just the icons. It looks cool the way it is, but it's more confusing.

The display should remain on by default, and there should be a button to turn it on and off. The icons should be bigger and the remote should have its own display. The timer should be displayed as one number.

The timer should have smaller increments than 1 hour, and should have increase and decrease functionality instead of a cycle.

User Personas

Straightforward Samantha

Samantha is used to using a "regular fan" and is unconcerned with all of the "fancy settings." She is simply too warm and uncomfortable; she wants something that gets the job done without being needlessly complicated.

Precise Pete

Pete uses his fan almost every night; it is like a routine for him. He prioritizes a fan that caters to his exact specifications, and is willing to go through multiple steps to make that happen. He wants total control over the fan's settings, even at the cost of some extra effort.

User Storyboard: Precise Pete

It is 9:30 PM, and Pete wants to do 30 minutes of homework in his bed and then go to sleep, as is his nightly routine.

As usual, Pete's room is a bit too warm for his comfort.

Pete walks over to his fan and turns it on.

Even though he's had this fan for a while, he still gets tripped up by the similar look of the strength and mode buttons.

Pete toggles through the strength and mode settings with his hand in front of the fan to choose the best ones.

Pete grabs the remote, sits down on his bed, and prepares to begin his homework.

Noticing that the fan is not pointed directly at him, Pete decides to adjust its direction by turning on the swivel setting.

"That's better!" Once the fan is facing the right way, Pete turns off the swivel setting.

Now a bit further away from the fan, Pete adjusts the strength setting to increase its intensity.

Pete is done with his homework, and adjusts the fan to his "sleep settings." He turns on oscillation, night mode, and a 7-hour timer to conserve energy.

Pete goes to sleep. Night mode keeps the fan quiet, and the oscillation provides a nice, level cooling effect across the entire room without being overwhelming.

Pete wakes up 10 hours later. Due to the 7-hour timer he had set previously, the fan is turned off when he awakens.

Note: Interview responses are not exact quotes from the users; they are paraphrased and shortened into summaries. Users were told to at least experiment with each setting in order to experience the entirety of the interface (otherwise, the users probably would not have tried the timer in these short interviews).

Note: This interface is well-suited for both public and private view. User interviews and observations were conducted in public view; the storyboard takes place in private view because that scenario better aligns with Precise Pete's archetypal use-case. The photos in the storyboard are my own recreation of Precise Pete's hypothetical usage of the product.
